Approach to the Dizzy Patient

Using the TiTrATE algorithm for recent-onset dizziness: timing, triggers, and targeted bedside examination.

TiTrATE Algorithm

Algorithm Path

    This app follows TiTrATE logic for recent-onset dizziness. It is educational decision support and does not replace emergency evaluation or clinician judgment.

    New case

    Instruction Panel

    • TimingClassify the dizziness by duration and pattern: episodic attacks versus continuous symptoms lasting hours to days.
    • TriggersA true trigger reliably starts an attack from baseline. Movement that merely worsens ongoing dizziness is an exacerbator.
    • Targeted examThe bedside test depends on the vestibular syndrome: positional tests for triggered episodic cases, HINTS-family exam for acute vestibular syndrome.
    • ABCD2 risk factorsAge 60 years or older, blood pressure at presentation at least 140 systolic or 90 diastolic, clinical features of unilateral weakness or speech disturbance, symptom duration, and diabetes. Scores range from 0 to 7; in the TiTrATE map, ABCD2 greater than 3 increases concern for TIA/stroke workup.

    Dix-Hallpike Maneuver

    Use when the story is brief, position-triggered episodic dizziness, especially rolling in bed, looking up, or bending. It tests mainly for posterior canal BPPV.

    1. Start with the patient seated upright on the exam table, legs extended or ready to be lowered backward.
    2. Turn the head 45 degrees toward the ear you are testing.
    3. Support the head and quickly bring the patient back to a supine head-hanging position, with the neck extended about 20 degrees below horizontal.
    4. Keep the eyes open and observe for at least 30 to 60 seconds. Ask whether this reproduces the typical spell.
    5. Bring the patient back to sitting and observe again; then repeat on the other side if needed.
    Peripheral BPPV patternBrief latency, transient upbeat-torsional nystagmus, fatigues, usually resolves within 60 seconds.
    Concerning patternPersistent, downbeat, purely vertical, direction-changing, or non-fatiguing nystagmus suggests central positional vertigo.
    ObservationExpected / normalAbnormal pattern
    SymptomsNo vertigo or only nonspecific discomfort.Typical brief vertigo reproduced by the maneuver.
    LatencyNo nystagmus.Posterior canal BPPV often has a short latency before nystagmus begins.
    NystagmusAbsent.Upbeat-torsional nystagmus toward the lower ear supports posterior canal BPPV.
    DurationNo provoked nystagmus.Canalithiasis pattern is transient, commonly under 60 seconds; persistent or vertical/downbeat patterns are concerning.
    Do not use casually: Avoid or modify when neck instability, severe cervical disease, suspected vertebrobasilar insufficiency, or inability to tolerate head extension is present.

    Supine Roll Test

    Use when brief positional attacks suggest horizontal canal BPPV, especially if Dix-Hallpike is negative or horizontal nystagmus is suspected.

    1. Place the patient supine and flex the head about 20 to 30 degrees so the horizontal canals are aligned.
    2. Rapidly turn the head 90 degrees to one side and observe the eyes for horizontal nystagmus and symptom reproduction.
    3. Return the head to center and let symptoms settle.
    4. Rapidly turn the head 90 degrees to the opposite side and compare intensity and direction.
    Geotropic patternNystagmus beats toward the ground; the stronger side often indicates the affected ear.
    Apogeotropic patternNystagmus beats away from the ground; interpretation differs and may require vestibular expertise.
    ObservationExpected / normalAbnormal pattern
    SymptomsNo typical vertigo provoked by either head turn.Brief vertigo reproduced when rolling the head to one or both sides.
    NystagmusNo horizontal positional nystagmus.Horizontal geotropic or apogeotropic nystagmus suggests horizontal canal BPPV.
    Side comparisonNo meaningful asymmetry.Intensity differs by side; the stronger side helps localize in geotropic canalithiasis.

    Orthostatic Vital Signs

    Use when standing reliably triggers lightheadedness, faintness, or presyncope. This is a triggered episodic pathway, but not a positional vertigo maneuver.

    1. Have the patient rest supine for about 5 minutes when feasible.
    2. Measure blood pressure and pulse while supine.
    3. Have the patient stand safely with support available.
    4. Repeat blood pressure and pulse at about 1 minute and again at 3 minutes.
    5. Record symptoms during the measurement: dizziness, faintness, visual dimming, palpitations, or weakness.
    Supports orthostasisSymptoms are reproduced with a significant blood pressure drop or abnormal pulse response.
    Think broaderSyncope, exertional symptoms, chest pain, or palpitations should prompt cardiac or systemic evaluation.
    MeasureExpected / normalAbnormal threshold
    Systolic BPFalls less than 20 mm Hg after standing.Drop of 20 mm Hg or more within 3 minutes supports orthostatic hypotension.
    Diastolic BPFalls less than 10 mm Hg after standing.Drop of 10 mm Hg or more within 3 minutes supports orthostatic hypotension.
    Heart rate responseMild compensatory rise can occur after standing.With orthostatic BP drop, a small HR rise may suggest neurogenic OH; a larger compensatory rise suggests volume depletion or non-neurogenic causes.
    POTS screen in adultsNo sustained excessive tachycardia with standing.Sustained HR increase of 30 bpm or more within 10 minutes without orthostatic hypotension suggests POTS physiology.

    HINTS Plus

    Use only for spontaneous acute vestibular syndrome: continuous dizziness or vertigo, nausea/gait unsteadiness, and spontaneous nystagmus while symptomatic. It is not for resolved spells or isolated brief positional dizziness.

    1. Head impulse: Ask the patient to fixate on your nose. Turn the head quickly and unpredictably about 10 to 20 degrees to each side. A corrective catch-up saccade suggests a peripheral vestibular deficit; a normal head impulse in true AVS is concerning for stroke.
    2. Nystagmus: Observe gaze in primary position, left gaze, and right gaze. Unidirectional horizontal nystagmus fits a peripheral pattern; direction-changing, vertical, or torsional-dominant nystagmus is central until proven otherwise.
    3. Test of skew: Perform alternate cover testing while the patient fixes on a target. A vertical refixation movement when uncovering either eye suggests skew deviation and is concerning for a central lesion.
    4. Plus hearing: Check for new unilateral hearing loss using finger rub, whispered voice, tuning fork, or audiometry when available. New hearing loss with AVS can be vascular.
    5. Gait/truncal stability: Assess ability to sit, stand, or walk safely. Severe truncal ataxia or inability to sit/stand unaided is a central warning sign.
    Peripheral patternAbnormal head impulse, unidirectional horizontal nystagmus, no skew, no new unilateral hearing loss.
    Central patternNormal head impulse, direction-changing/vertical nystagmus, skew, new hearing loss, focal signs, or severe ataxia.
    HINTS plus componentPeripheral / lower-risk AVS patternCentral / abnormal warning pattern
    Head impulseAbnormal toward the affected side, with corrective catch-up saccade.Normal head impulse in true AVS is concerning for stroke.
    NystagmusUnidirectional horizontal or horizontal-torsional nystagmus.Direction-changing gaze-evoked, vertical, or pure torsional nystagmus.
    Test of skewNo vertical refixation on alternate cover test.Skew deviation present: vertical refixation movement when either eye is uncovered.
    Plus hearingNo new unilateral hearing loss.New unilateral hearing loss with AVS can indicate AICA/posterior circulation ischemia.
    Truncal/gait stabilityCan sit and stand with expected imbalance for peripheral vestibulopathy.Cannot sit or stand unaided, severe truncal ataxia, focal neurologic signs.
    Key safety point: HINTS plus depends on correct patient selection and examiner skill. If the exam is uncertain, incomplete, or the patient has central features, treat as urgent until evaluated properly.

    Why TiTrATE starts with timing

    Traditional symptom labels like vertigo, lightheadedness, or disequilibrium are less reliable than the syndrome pattern. TiTrATE first asks whether symptoms are episodic or continuous.

    Why triggers matter

    Brief dizziness that starts only after a positional change points to triggered episodic syndromes. Continuous dizziness worsened by motion is not the same thing.

    When the result is urgent

    Central eye findings, focal neurologic signs, severe truncal ataxia, or an acute vestibular syndrome with a central HINTS pattern should prompt emergency stroke evaluation.
